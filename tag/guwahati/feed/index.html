Environmentalists around the world are faced by the same silly responses every time there’s a cry for saving the nature around us, “we don’t have time”, “who cares?” or “go to someone who loves nature better!”  The motivation to protect comes from our feeling of empathy towards the subject, but one cannot empathize with anything if they are not even familiar with it!

Polluting the Beautiful

Over the years, much publicity has been done to highlight the issues that have plagued the wildlife, but similar extent of awareness in public has not been achieved with regards to the life of these beautiful creations. Most would not like to be presented with problems of those whom they never knew. Would you go out of your way to fix a leaking roof of a guy few blocks away and whom you never met? The answer is mostly no.

Perhaps, what we can do is to include wildlife in school curriculum as something that is fun activity and inculcate the habit of understanding environment from early childhood. Our media can try to cover more and more about nature, its beauty and its impact than just printing small article on some conservation measure in page 4. Its a Catch 22 situation, media covers what people will like to see and people see what media shows them.

People do not miss nature enough to care for its saving, we can change it by making nature an integral part of everyone’s life and make them realize what’s at stake of being lost forever. – Avinash Hindupur

The Common Birds are called as Columba livia from Latin which was in turn derived from Greek. Columba is from kolumbos, a diver while livia is obtained from peleia ie. Greek for dove, supposedly derived from pellos dark colored referring to the coloring of these birds!

Common Moorhens are found in swamps, fresh-water bodies and tanks with some green vegetation throughout India. These playful and open birds are easily recognized by their bright pink frontal shield on their completely slate black body with running white lines on the flanks. These birds are also seen in many mixed groups of waterfowl like Whistling ducks, White-breasted Waterhens and Pochards.

Common Moorhens can be confused with Watercocks from distance which look similar in appearance. The distinctive identifier of Common Moorhens in this case is smaller frontal shield and white bands on the flanks. For other waterbirds seen visit this checklist!

Meaning of Scientific Name:

Common Moorhen is called Gallinula chloropus, named from Latin and Greek as in many cases. Gallinula means a small hen or chicken, referring to the size and shape of the species in Latin, while cholorpus is derived from Greek Chloros and pous meaning green and foot. This refers to the strange green foot these birds have which might have come across as something distinctive in early days of naming.

Common Name: Common Moorhen                                            Scientific Name: Gallinula chloropus

Local Names: Assamese:                                                                       Size: 30-35 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Slake black body

2. Yellowish lime bill

3. Pink frontal shield

4. Green legs

5. White running lines on flanks.

Brown Shrikes are the only species of Shrikes found naturally other than Long-tailed Shrikes and Grey-backed Shrikes. These birds arrive early winter every year and can be seen till late into March or early April. Easily the smallest among the three species, Brown Shrikes are easily recognizable even from a distance by their complete brown coloring and unmistakable black band across the eyes.

Most often seen alone on perched on a branch, the Brown Shrike exhibits habits of the Shrike family in general, looking for a prey from a height and then hanging their prey on thorny bushes. For other birds that give company to these butchers of the wild in our IIT Guwahati, visit this checklist!

Meaning of Scientific Name:

Lanius is derived from Latin laniare meaning to tear to pieces, characteristic of butchers, describing the general characteristic of the Shrike family of hanging their prey on thorns.  But, in general ornithological discussions, lanius is also used to mean birds with stout hooked bills or for those that look similar to shrikes. Thus, historically Shrikes were also known as Butcher Birds. Cristatus means crested or plumed in Latin referring to the coloring of the bird which was described as Russet ( a shade of brown) or Crested Red originally.

Common Name: Brown Shrike                                             Scientific Name: Lanius Cristatus

Local Names: Assamese:                                                                       Size: 16 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Brown body

2. Black band on eyes

3. White supercilium

4. Whitish brown underparts

5. White throat

Rufous Treepie is the largest and by far the most common resident Treepie found in India and is found only in Indo-Malayan ecozone. These Treepies look a lot like Magpies in shape and belong to Crow family in general. While these are generally found in open wooded forests, one can easily spot one after first glance by their long tail, contrasting color and high metallic calls.

Rufous Treepies like other arboreal birds such as Malkohas are very good climbers and can be seen going out of site by quickly climbing through the branches. These were observed to feed on insects, fruits and nuts under normal circumstances. For other birds found in nearby places, visit this checklist!

Meaning of Scientific Name:

The scientific name of this species describes its behavior and habitat in general with both Latin and Greek used to name it. Dendrocitta is Greek and has two components: Dendron meaning tree and kitta means Magpie. Vagabunda is latin for wandering. Thus, the name implies that this species is a “tree wandering magpie”!

Common Name: Rufous Treepie                                             Scientific Name: Dendrocitta vagabunda

Local Names:                                                                                   Size: 45-50 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Long silver tail with black tip

2. Cinnamon brown colored upper body

3. White-grey band on black wings

4. Dark grey colored head and throat

5. Red eyes with black iris

Plaintive Cuckoos are one of the smaller Cuckoos found in India and are seen only in the eastern and northeastern parts of the country. These birds are not easily visible and are generally considered shy dwelling near farms, open forests and similar habitats feeding mainly on insects and worms. As like other cuckoos, Plaintive Cuckoo females occur in hepatic morphs looking dark brown with regular barrings

Origin of Scientific Name:

The scientific name of the bird gives an interesting explanation for those interested in academic and history trivia, Cacomantis means the prophet of evil in ancient Greek (Kakos mantis : inner malice prophet) because the ancient superstitious Javanese considered these as prophets of doom as these usually called from graveyards in the night and merulinus in medieval Latin means thrush like describing its appearance in color.

Common Name: Plaintive Cuckoo                                             Scientific Name: Cacomantis merulinus

Local Names: Assamese:                                                                       Size: 23-25 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Grey head and throat

2. Whitish orange underparts

3. Red eyes

4. Dull rufuscent brown upperparts

5. Barred tail

Striated Grassbirds are easily the largest Grassbirds found in India. The scientific name Megalurus palustris means Long-tailed Marsh living bird, thus explaining significantly about its habits and habitat and appearance. The Striated Grassbirds have long pointed tail with streaking on their dull brown body. These are generally noticed first for their high-pitched whistle calls, but are otherwise completely camouflaged in their habitat of brown grass and reeds.

These grassbirds have been observed to adopt to reeds/ grass or wetland habitats with variations in food hunt in both cases. One can observe that these birds fly up and down over a same place for quite some time catching insects while making the high pitched noises. Found mainly in central and north as well as north east India, these birds are little studied till date. Check out our complete checklist here!

Common Kingfishers are amongst the three most common Kingfishers found all over India along with White-throated Kingfisher and Pied Kingfisher. Comparatively very small in Kingfishers, its their high pitched voice that first attracts the birder and later its bright colors give it away to the keen eyes. The only other bird that can lead to confusion with this bird in India is the Blue-eared Kingfisher which looks similar but with blue ear coverts.

If closely observed for sometime, one can see that this small Kingfisher swings in its branch up and down in a strange manner before suddenly plunging into water from a height of 2-3 m and coming back with tiny prey.  For most of the time, these birds were observed to be solitary and strong defenders of their territories with warning calls and actions. Black-eared Kites are proposed species split of the Black Kites, which are permanent residents in Indian Sub-continent. These Black-eared Kites, which can be seen mainly in winters, have prominent broad white patch under their wings which is variable in Black Kites. This whistling raptor can be seen circling in skies of cities, town and general human habitats looking for small prey.

Black-eared Kites share a very distinct identifier with Black Kites – their forked tail, which can easily distinguish them from eagles, hawks and vultures when soaring high. Have a look at other birds sighted in this checklist.

Local Names: Assamese:                                                                Size: 57-67 cms, wingspan of 130-150 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Rufous body

2. Wings with black edges, looking arched while in flight

3. Beak with base in yellow and ending with black

4. Prominent and large white wing patch (from Black Kites)

Jungle Mynas are fast becoming a common sight outside their native range of lower Himalayas, east India and Western Ghats within India. Biologically very similar to its close relative and ‘Most Invasive Species’ Common Myna, these jungle counterparts have adapted to cultivation and settlements! As the Jungle Mynas are opportunistic feeders, they eat anything from food scrap to insects to fruit and nectar. For other species which share the same environmental niche, visit this checklist!

Jungle Mynas can be typically seen in company with other Starling family members like Common Mynas and Asian Pied Starlings. When in communal roost, Mynas can be very noisy before leaving for foraging and after coming back home. The ideal time for watching these fierce eyed beauties is early in morning when they are ready for the day and have a bath one by one!

 

Common Name: Jungle Myna                                                                        Scientific Name: Acridotheres fuscus

Local Names: Assamese:                                                                                  Size: 20-23 cms

Global Status: Least Threatened                                                                    Local Status: ~ 100

 

Distinctive Identifiers:

1. Prominent frontal crest

2. white patch at base of primary feathers

3. white tail tip

4. Orange bill with blue base

5. Darker gray coloration for overall body
